
# 6. Slide Annotations & Leftover Rules

These are specific remarks from the slides (Slides 45, 53, 58) that serve as absolute rules for your modeling.

### 1. N-ary Associations are Temporary (Slide 45)
* **The Rule:** *"La plupart du temps nous nous en servons que pour esquisser la modélisation au début du projet, puis elle est vite remplacée par un ensemble d'associations binaires afin de lever toute ambiguïté."*
* **What this means for exams:** If you are asked to provide a final Class Diagram for a software architecture or database generation, **do not leave a ternary association (diamond) in your diagram.** Always convert it into an intermediate class with `1` to `*` associations.

### 2. The "Cheval" (Horse) Anomaly - Both Aggregation and Composition (Slide 58)
* **The Scenario:** Can a class be part of an Aggregation AND a Composition at the same time? Yes.
* **Example:** `Cheval` (Horse). 
  * A Horse is *Composed* (`*--`) of a `Tête` (Head) and 4 `Jambes` (Legs). If the horse dies, the legs and head die.
  * A Horse *Aggregates* (`o--`) a `Selle` (Saddle). The saddle is part of the "Ridden Horse" concept, but if the horse dies, you take the saddle off and use it on another horse.
* **Exam Application:** Do not be afraid to attach multiple different diamond types to the same central class if the text justifies it.

### 3. Resolving Association Classes Multiplicity (Slide 50/54)
When you convert an Association Class into a normal intermediate class (like converting the `Note` between `Etudiant` and `Examen`), the multiplicities flip in a very specific way.
* **Before:** `Etudiant "1..*"` ----- `Examen "0..*"` (with `Note` hanging off).
* **After:** `Etudiant "1"` <-- `"*"` `Note` `"*"` --> `"1"` `Examen`.
* **The Absolute Rule:** The intermediate class ALWAYS gets the `*` side of the multiplicities. The original classes ALWAYS get `1` (or `0..1`). Writing `*` next to `Etudiant` in the resolved diagram is an instant zero for that relationship.
