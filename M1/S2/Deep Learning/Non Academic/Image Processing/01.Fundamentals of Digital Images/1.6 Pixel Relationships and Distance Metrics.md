# 1.6 Pixel Relationships and Distance Metrics

Understanding how pixels relate to their neighbors is fundamental for spatial filtering, segmentation, and morphology.

## 1. Neighbors of a Pixel
For a coordinate $(x,y)$, which surrounding pixels matter?

### A. 4-Neighbors ($N_4$)
The pixels that share an **edge** with $(x,y)$.
*   Coordinates: $(x+1, y), (x-1, y), (x, y+1), (x, y-1)$.
*   Locations: Top, Bottom, Left, Right.
*   Visual: A "Cross" shape.

### B. 8-Neighbors ($N_8$)
The pixels that share an **edge OR a corner** with $(x,y)$.
*   Coordinates: $N_4$ plus the 4 diagonals: $(x+1, y+1), (x+1, y-1), (x-1, y+1), (x-1, y-1)$.
*   Visual: A "Square" surrounding the pixel.

## 2. Adjacency and Connectivity
Simply being next to each other isn't enough. For two pixels to be "connected" (part of the same object), they usually need to share a property (e.g., color).

Let $V$ be the set of intensity values that define "adjacency" (e.g., $V=\{1\}$ for binary white pixels).

1.  **4-Adjacency:** Two pixels $p$ and $q$ with values from $V$ are 4-adjacent if $q$ is in the set $N_4(p)$.
2.  **8-Adjacency:** Two pixels $p$ and $q$ with values from $V$ are 8-adjacent if $q$ is in the set $N_8(p)$.

**The Connectivity Paradox:**
Using 8-adjacency can sometimes lead to ambiguity where an object appears connected and separated at the same time depending on interpretation (common in thin diagonal lines). This is solved by **m-adjacency**, but for this level, understanding 4 vs 8 is sufficient.

## 3. Distance Metrics
How far apart are two pixels $p(x,y)$ and $q(s,t)$? In continuous math, there is one answer. In a digital grid, there are three.

### A. Euclidean Distance ($D_e$)
The straight-line distance "as the crow flies."
$$ D_e(p, q) = \sqrt{(x-s)^2 + (y-t)^2} $$
*   **Shape:** Circles. Points equidistant from the center form a circle.
*   **Pros:** Most accurate geometrically.
*   **Cons:** Computationally expensive (square roots are slow for computers).

### B. City-Block Distance ($D_4$ or Manhattan)
The distance if you can only move horizontally and vertically (like a taxi in Manhattan).
$$ D_4(p, q) = |x-s| + |y-t| $$
*   **Shape:** Diamonds. Points equidistant from the center form a diamond shape.
*   **Logic:** Corresponds to counting steps in the $N_4$ neighborhood.

### C. Chessboard Distance ($D_8$ or Chebyshev)
The distance if you can move in any of the 8 directions (like a King in Chess).
$$ D_8(p, q) = \max(|x-s|, |y-t|) $$
*   **Shape:** Squares. Points equidistant from the center form a square.
*   **Logic:** Corresponds to counting steps in the $N_8$ neighborhood. The diagonal move counts as distance 1, just like a horizontal move.

### Comparative Example
Consider $p(0,0)$ and $q(3,3)$.
*   **Euclidean:** $\sqrt{3^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{18} \approx 4.24$
*   **City-Block:** $|3| + |3| = 6$ (3 right, 3 down).
*   **Chessboard:** $\max(3, 3) = 3$ (3 diagonal moves).

**Insight:** Notice that $D_8 \leq D_e \leq D_4$. The Chessboard distance is always the "shortest" integer path because diagonal moves are highly efficient.
