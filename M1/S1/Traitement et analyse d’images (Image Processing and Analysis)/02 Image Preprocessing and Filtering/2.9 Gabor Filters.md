

Gabor filters are specialized linear filters found in the frequency and spatial domains. They are unique because they are mathematically similar to how the mammalian primary visual cortex processes visual information.

## 1. Definition

A Gabor filter is essentially a **Gaussian Kernel** modulated by a **Sinusoidal Plane Wave**.

*   **Gaussian Part:** Localizes the filter (limits it to a small area).
*   **Sinusoidal Part:** Selects a specific frequency and orientation.

## 2. The Formula (Slide 71)

$$ G(x, y) = \exp \left( -\frac{x'^2 + \gamma^2 y'^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) \cdot \cos \left( 2\pi \frac{x'}{\lambda} + \psi \right) $$

*(Note: The slides use a slightly different notation involving $W, \theta, \phi$, but the components are the same).*

### Key Parameters:
1.  **$\lambda$ (Wavelength/Frequency):** Determines the "scale" or "coarseness" of the texture to detect.
2.  **$\theta$ (Orientation):** The angle of the filter. A Gabor filter at $90^\circ$ detects horizontal lines; at $0^\circ$, it detects vertical lines.
3.  **$\sigma$ (Sigma):** The size of the Gaussian envelope (how big the filter is).
4.  **$\gamma$ (Aspect Ratio):** The ellipticity of the filter.

## 3. Applications

Gabor filters are rarely used for simple noise reduction. They are used for **Analysis** and **Extraction**:

1.  **Texture Analysis:** Because textures have repetitive patterns (frequencies) at specific angles, a "Bank of Gabor Filters" (filters with different angles and frequencies) can identify what kind of texture is present.
2.  **Edge Detection:** Can detect edges at specific orientations.
3.  **Biometrics:** Iris recognition and fingerprint enhancement rely heavily on Gabor filters to extract unique directional patterns.

## 4. Visual Representation

If you look at a Gabor filter kernel:
*   It looks like a series of parallel light and dark stripes (the wave).
*   The stripes fade out towards the edges (the Gaussian).
