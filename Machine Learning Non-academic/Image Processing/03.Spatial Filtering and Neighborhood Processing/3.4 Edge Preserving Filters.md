# 3.4 Edge Preserving Filters

Standard smoothing filters blur noise AND edges. Advanced filters attempt to smooth noise only in flat areas while "stopping" at edges.

## 1. The Nagao Filter
An adaptive filter that searches for the "best" neighborhood.

### The Algorithm
For a $5 \times 5$ window, it defines 9 overlapping sub-regions (representing corners, lines, and center).
1.  Calculate the **Variance** ($\sigma^2$) of each of the 9 sub-regions.
2.  Find the sub-region with the **Minimum Variance** (the "calmest" or most uniform area).
3.  Calculate the **Mean** of only that specific sub-region.
4.  Replace the center pixel with that mean.

**Logic:** If the center pixel is near an edge, the sub-regions crossing the edge will have high variance. The sub-regions sitting entirely on one side of the edge will have low variance. By choosing the low-variance one, the filter never "blurs across" the edge.

## 2. The Bilateral Filter
The most popular edge-preserving smoother in modern photography.

It uses two Gaussian weights combined:
1.  **Spatial Weight ($G_s$):** Based on geometric distance (standard Gaussian). Closer pixels have more weight.
2.  **Range Weight ($G_r$):** Based on intensity difference. Pixels with a similar color to the center have more weight.

**Result:** A pixel that is physically close but has a vastly different color (like across an edge) is ignored.

## 3. Anisotropic Diffusion (Perona-Malik)
Based on the Physics of Heat Diffusion.
*   It treats the image like a metal plate where heat (intensity) spreads over time.
*   The "Conductivity" is high in flat areas (fast smoothing).
*   The "Conductivity" is set to zero at edges (heat cannot cross the edge).
*   **Visual:** Produces a "cartoon" effect where colors are flat but boundaries are razor-sharp.
