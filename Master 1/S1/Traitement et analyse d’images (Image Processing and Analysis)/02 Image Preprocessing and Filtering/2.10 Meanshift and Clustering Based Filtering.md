## 1. Meanshift Filtering

Meanshift is a non-parametric iterative algorithm used to locate the maxima of a density function. In image processing, it smooths images by forcing each pixel to "climb" to the nearest "mode" (peak density of color) in its neighborhood.

### The Algorithm
For a pixel $x$, the algorithm finds the mean of all pixels within a search window (bandwidth $h$).
1.  Define a window around $x$.
2.  Compute the **mean position** and **mean color** of pixels in that window.
3.  Move the window center to this new mean ($m(x)$).
4.  Repeat until convergence (the window stops moving).
5.  Assign the final "mode" color to the original pixel.

**Formula for the Shift Vector $m(x)$:**
$$ m(x) = \frac{\sum x_i K(x-x_i)}{\sum K(x-x_i)} - x $$
*(Where $K$ is a kernel function).*

### Result
The image becomes composed of flat regions of constant color with sharp boundaries. It looks like a posterized or segmented image.

## 2. 2SP-DBSCAN (UMBB Research)

The slides highlight a specific contribution from the **iVision team at UMBB** (Prof. Gaceb, Z. Kezzoula). This method improves upon standard clustering for document cleaning.

### Concept
*   **Standard DBSCAN:** A density-based clustering algorithm. It groups points that are closely packed together.
*   **Problem:** Standard DBSCAN is slow and doesn't handle image spatial constraints well.
*   **2SP (Two-Space) DBSCAN:**
    *   Combines **Spatial** information (x, y coordinates) + **Color** information (R, G, B or Grayscale).
    *   Uses **Integral Volumes** to accelerate the calculation.


### Workflow

1. **Low-Level:** Image Segmentation.
    
2. **High-Level:** Clustering pixels.
    
3. **Application:** Document restoration (removing background noise/artifacts/stains while keeping text sharp).
    
4. **Result:** As seen in Slide 123-126, it removes paper textures and stains from historical documents better than standard methods.
