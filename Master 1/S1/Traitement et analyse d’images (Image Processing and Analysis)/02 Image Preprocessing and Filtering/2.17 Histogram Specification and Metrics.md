# 2.15 Histogram Specification and Metrics

## 1. Histogram Specification (Matching)

Sometimes we don't want a "flat" uniform histogram (Equalization). We might want the histogram to look like a specific shape (e.g., matching the lighting of a reference movie scene).

*   **Goal:** Transform the histogram of Image $A$ so it matches the histogram of Image $B$.
*   **Method:**
    1.  Equalize Image $A$ (Find transformation $T_1$).
    2.  Equalize Target Image $B$ (Find transformation $T_2$).
    3.  The mapping from $A \to B$ is $T_2^{-1}(T_1(A))$. (Inverse transformation).

### Thang & Zhuang Method (Slide 185)
The slides present a specific mathematical curve for modifying histograms to improve contrast without washing out details.
*   **Formula:** $F(x) = 0.5 + G(x - m)$
*   **Component:** $G(x) = \frac{\arctan(\delta \cdot x)}{\pi}$
*   **Insight:** This uses an arctangent curve (S-curve). S-curves are famous in image processing for boosting contrast in mid-tones while compressing shadows and highlights slightly, protecting them from clipping.

## 2. Histogram Similarity Metrics (Slide 187)

How do we mathematically prove two images have similar lighting or distributions? We compare their histograms ($h_1$ and $h_2$).

### A. Intersection
Calculates the overlapping area between the two histograms.
$$ d(h_1, h_2) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \min(h_1(i), h_2(i)) $$
*   High value = Strong similarity.

### B. Correlation
Statistical correlation between the two plots.
$$ d(h_1, h_2) = \frac{\sum (h_1(i) - \bar{h_1})(h_2(i) - \bar{h_2})}{\sqrt{\sum (h_1(i) - \bar{h_1})^2 \sum (h_2(i) - \bar{h_2})^2}} $$
*   1 = Identical, 0 = No correlation, -1 = Inverse.

### C. Chi-Square ($\chi^2$)
Measures the distance/divergence. Often used in texture matching.
$$ d(h_1, h_2) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{[h_1(i) - h_2(i)]^2}{h_1(i) + h_2(i)} $$
*   **Low value (0)** = Perfect match.
