
# 2.7 Anisotropic Diffusion (Perona-Malik)

Anisotropic diffusion is a technique based on Partial Differential Equations (PDEs). Instead of using a fixed mask convolution, it treats the image as a physical system where intensity "diffuses" (spreads) over time, like heat.

## 1. The Core Idea

*   **Isotropic Diffusion (Standard Gaussian):** Heat (intensity) spreads equally in all directions. Edges get blurred.
*   **Anisotropic Diffusion:** Heat spreads in smooth areas but is **blocked** by "insulators" (edges).

## 2. The Perona-Malik Equation

The evolution of the image $I$ over time $t$ is given by:

$$ \frac{\partial I}{\partial t} = \text{div}( c(x,y,t) \cdot \nabla I ) $$

*   **$\nabla I$ (Gradient):** Represents the edges. High gradient = Strong edge.
*   **$c(x,y,t)$ (Conductance Coefficient):** This is the "control valve."
    *   If Gradient is **Low** (Flat region) $\rightarrow$ $c$ is High $\rightarrow$ Diffuse (Smooth).
    *   If Gradient is **High** (Edge) $\rightarrow$ $c$ is Low $\rightarrow$ Stop Diffusion (Preserve Edge).

### Diffusion Functions
Perona and Malik proposed two functions for $c(|\nabla I|)$:
1.  **Exponential:** Good for high-contrast edges.
    $$ c(\nabla I) = e^{-(\|\nabla I\|/K)^2} $$
2.  **Quadratic:** Good for wide regions over smoother edges.
    $$ c(\nabla I) = \frac{1}{1 + (\|\nabla I\|/K)^2} $$

*   **$K$ (Kappa):** A sensitivity threshold. Gradients larger than $K$ are treated as edges (diffusion stops). Gradients smaller than $K$ are treated as noise (diffusion continues).

## 3. The Algorithm (Discrete Implementation)

In practice, we solve this iteratively (Slide 129):

$$ I_{t+1} = I_t + \lambda \sum_{p \in \eta} g(\nabla I) \cdot \nabla I $$

Where:
*   $\lambda$: Speed of diffusion (usually $\le 0.25$ for stability).
*   $t$: Number of iterations. More iterations = smoother image (cartoon effect).

## 4. Pros and Cons

*   **Pros:** Produces "cartoon-like" images with very smooth regions and extremely sharp boundaries. Excellent for segmentation preprocessing.
*   **Cons:**
    *   **Staircasing:** Smooth gradients (like a sky) can turn into flat blocks (stairs).
    *   **Iterative:** Slow to compute.
    *   **Instability:** Sensitive to the choice of $K$ and $\lambda$.
