# 2.12 Deep Learning for Image Denoising

Traditional filters (Gaussian, Median, Bilateral) rely on hand-crafted mathematical models. Deep Learning (DL) approaches learn the noise model and the image structure directly from data (pairs of noisy and clean images).

## 1. Why Deep Learning?
*   **Universal:** Can adapt to complex, non-Gaussian noise distributions that traditional filters fail to handle.
*   **Context:** Unlike local filters (3x3 or 5x5 windows), DL models can consider larger contexts or even the whole image.
*   **Performance:** Generally achieves significantly higher PSNR and SSIM scores.

## 2. MLP Based Denoising (The Early Era)
*Reference: Burger et al. 2012 (Slide 138)*

*   **Architecture:** A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP).
*   **Input:** An image patch (e.g., 17x17 pixels) flattened into a vector.
*   **Structure:** Several fully connected hidden layers.
*   **Output:** The clean patch.
*   **Limitation:** It ignores the 2D spatial structure of images because it flattens patches into vectors. It is computationally heavy for large images.

## 3. CNN and Auto-Encoders (The Modern Era)
*Reference: Zhang et al. 2017 (DnCNN)*

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) preserve spatial relationships. A common architecture for restoration is the **Auto-Encoder** or **U-Net** style (Hourglass shape).

### Architecture Flow:
1.  **Encoder (Contraction):** Series of Convolutions and Pooling. Extracts high-level features and reduces spatial resolution.
2.  **Bottleneck (Latent Space):** Compressed representation of the image content.
3.  **Decoder (Expansion):** Transposed Convolutions (Upsampling). Reconstructs the image from features.
4.  **Skip Connections (U-Net specific):** Direct links between Encoder and Decoder layers to preserve fine details lost during pooling.

## 4. Residual Learning (Crucial Concept)

This is the most important theoretical leap in DL denoising (Slide 142).

*   **Standard Learning:** Input Noisy Image ($Y$) $\rightarrow$ Network $\rightarrow$ Output Clean Image ($X$).
    *   *Problem:* The network has to "memorize" the image structure, which is hard.
*   **Residual Learning:** Input Noisy Image ($Y$) $\rightarrow$ Network $\rightarrow$ Output **The Noise** ($V$).
    *   *Why?* The noise map is usually sparser and easier to learn than the complex image texture.
*   **Final Step:** Simply subtract the learned noise from the input:
    $$ X_{clean} = Y_{input} - \text{Network}(Y_{input}) $$
*   **Loss Function:** Usually L2 norm (Mean Squared Error) between the predicted noise and the real noise.
    $$ L(Y, V) = || Y - V ||^2 $$

## 5. Transformers in Restoration (Uformer)
*Reference: Wang et al. 2021 (Slide 144)*

While CNNs are excellent, they are **local** (they look at small windows). **Transformers** (originally from NLP) use **Self-Attention** mechanisms.

*   **Advantage:** Self-attention allows the network to compare a pixel with **every other pixel** in the image (global context). This helps in reconstructing repetitive textures or patterns that are far apart.
*   **Uformer:** A U-Shaped architecture (like U-Net) but uses Transformer blocks instead of standard Convolution blocks.

## 6. Summary Comparison

| Method | Type | Strength | Weakness |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **MLP** | Fully Connected | Simple to implement. | Loses spatial info, slow for full images. |
| **CNN (Auto-Encoder)** | Convolutional | Preserves spatial info, fast inference. | Can sometimes over-smooth textures. |
| **Residual CNN (DnCNN)** | Residual Learning | Very easy to train, state-of-the-art performance. | Requires fixed noise level training (mostly). |
| **Transformers (Uformer)** | Attention Based | Global context, best texture recovery. | extremely heavy computation (VRAM usage). |
