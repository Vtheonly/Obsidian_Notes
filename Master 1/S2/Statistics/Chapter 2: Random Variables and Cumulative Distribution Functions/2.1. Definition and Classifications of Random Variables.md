# 2.1. Definition and Classifications of Random Variables

### 1. Mathematical Formulation
A **random variable** is not a single value, but rather a function that maps outcomes from a sample space to real numbers. This allows us to apply mathematical tools like algebra and calculus to analyze random experiments.

Let $(\Omega_1, \mathcal{A}_1, P)$ be a probability space, and let $(\Omega_2, \mathcal{A}_2)$ be a measurable space (typically, $\Omega_2 = \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathcal{A}_2 = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, where $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is the Borel $\sigma$-algebra on the real line). 

A mapping $X: \Omega_1 \to \Omega_2$ is a **random variable** if and only if it is a measurable function. This means the preimage of any measurable set $B \in \mathcal{A}_2$ must belong to the $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{A}_1$:
$$\forall B \in \mathcal{A}_2, \quad X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{A}_1$$
where the preimage is defined as:
$$X^{-1}(B) = \{\omega \in \Omega_1 \mid X(\omega) \in B\}$$

* **Why is measurability required?** We can only calculate the probability of events that belong to our $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{A}_1$. If the preimage $X^{-1}(B)$ lay outside $\mathcal{A}_1$, the probability $P(X \in B)$ would be undefined.

### 2. Classification of Random Variables
* **Discrete Random Variables:** The range of $X$ (denoted by $X(\Omega)$) is either finite or countably infinite (e.g., $\{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\}$ or $\{0, 1, 2, 3, \dots\}$).
* **Continuous Random Variables:** The range of $X$ is uncountably infinite (typically containing one or more intervals of real numbers, such as $[0, 1]$ or $[0, +\infty)$).
