### 1. Subject A: Multiple Choice Guessing Game (Bayes Solver)
* **Problem Statement:** A multiple-choice test has $c$ options per question. The probability that you know the correct answer to any given question is $p$. If you do not know the answer, you choose one at random.
  Determine the probability that you knew the answer, given that you answered correctly.

#### Formulating with Bayes' Theorem
We define the following events:
* $K$: "You know the answer" (with $P(K) = p$).
* $\bar{K}$: "You do not know the answer" (with $P(\bar{K}) = 1 - p$).
* $C$: "You answer correctly".

The conditional probabilities of answering correctly are:
* $P(C \mid K) = 1$ (if you know the answer, you answer correctly).
* $P(C \mid \bar{K}) = \frac{1}{c}$ (if you guess, you choose randomly among the $c$ options).

We wish to calculate the conditional probability $P(K \mid C)$:
$$P(K \mid C) = \frac{P(C \mid K)P(K)}{P(C \mid K)P(K) + P(C \mid \bar{K})P(\bar{K})}$$

Substituting the known probabilities:
$$P(K \mid C) = \frac{1 \cdot p}{1 \cdot p + \frac{1}{c}(1-p)} = \frac{p}{p + \frac{1-p}{c}}$$

Multiplying the numerator and denominator by $c$:
$$P(K \mid C) = \frac{pc}{pc + 1 - p}$$

### 2. Subject A: Doping Control (Binomial Model)
* **Problem Statement:** Before a marathon, 10 athletes are tested for doping. The probability of any athlete testing positive is $0.2$, and the tests are independent. Let $X$ represent the total number of athletes who test positive.
  1. Determine the probability distribution of $X$.
  2. Compute the probability that at least one athlete tests positive.
  3. Compute the expected number of positive tests.

#### Probability Distribution of $X$
Since we have a fixed number of independent trials ($n = 10$) with a constant success probability ($p = 0.2$), $X$ follows a binomial distribution:
$$X \sim \mathcal{B}(10, \quad 0.2)$$

The probability mass function is:
$$P(X = k) = \binom{10}{k} (0.2)^k (0.8)^{10-k} \quad \text{for } k \in \{0, 1, \dots, 10\}$$

#### Probability of at least one Positive Test
We can calculate this using the complement probability:
$$P(X \ge 1) = 1 - P(X = 0) = 1 - \binom{10}{0} (0.2)^0 (0.8)^{10} = 1 - (0.8)^{10} \approx 1 - 0.10737 = 0.89263$$

#### Expected Number of Positive Tests
$$E(X) = n \cdot p = 10 \cdot 0.2 = 2 \text{ athletes}$$

### 3. Subject A & B: Sequential Quality Control
* **Problem Statement:** A quality control test extracts parts one by one from a large batch and tests them. If a part is defective, the batch is rejected and testing stops. The maximum number of parts tested is 5.
  Let $X$ represent the total number of parts tested. Assuming the probability of a randomly chosen part being acceptable is $0.9$, find the probability distribution of $X$.

#### Analyzing the Outcomes for $X$
Let $A$ represent an acceptable part ($P(A) = 0.9$) and $D$ represent a defective part ($P(D) = 0.1$).

* **Case 1: $X = 1$**
  Testing stops after the first step if the first part is defective:
  $$P(X = 1) = P(D) = 0.1$$
* **Case 2: $X = 2$**
  Testing stops after the second step if the first part is acceptable and the second is defective:
  $$P(X = 2) = P(AD) = P(A)P(D) = 0.9 \times 0.1 = 0.09$$
* **Case 3: $X = 3$**
  Testing stops after the third step if the first two parts are acceptable and the third is defective:
  $$P(X = 3) = P(AAD) = P(A)^2 P(D) = (0.9)^2 \times 0.1 = 0.081$$
* **Case 4: $X = 4$**
  Testing stops after the fourth step if the first three parts are acceptable and the fourth is defective:
  $$P(X = 4) = P(AAAD) = P(A)^3 P(D) = (0.9)^3 \times 0.1 = 0.0729$$
* **Case 5: $X = 5$**
  Testing stops after 5 steps if either:
  * The first 4 parts are acceptable and the 5th is defective ($AAAAD$).
  * The first 4 parts are acceptable and the 5th is also acceptable ($AAAAA$).
  
  Since we always stop after testing 5 parts regardless of the outcome of the 5th test, this case is equivalent to the first 4 parts being acceptable:
  $$P(X = 5) = P(AAAA) = P(A)^4 = (0.9)^4 = 0.6561$$

#### Summary of the Probability Distribution
The complete PMF of $X$ is:

| $k$ | $P(X = k)$ |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1** | $0.1000$ |
| **2** | $0.0900$ |
| **3** | $0.0810$ |
| **4** | $0.0729$ |
| **5** | $0.6561$ |

We verify that the sum of the probabilities equals 1:
$$\sum_{k=1}^{5} P(X = k) = 0.1 + 0.09 + 0.081 + 0.0729 + 0.6561 = 1.0000$$

### 4. Subject B: Judicial Bribery (Bayes Application)
* **Problem Statement:** A court of law is suspected of taking bribes. If a detainee pays a bribe, they are freed. If they do not pay, the judges roll a fair six-sided die, and the detainee is freed if an even number appears.
  Let $q$ represent the probability that a detainee pays a bribe. Determine the probability that the judges were bribed, given that the detainee was freed.

#### Formulating with Bayes' Theorem
We define the following events:
* $B$: "The judges were bribed" (with $P(B) = q$).
* $\bar{B}$: "The judges were not bribed" (with $P(\bar{B}) = 1-q$).
* $F$: "The detainee is freed".

The conditional probabilities of the detainee being freed are:
* $P(F \mid B) = 1$ (if the judges are bribed, the detainee is freed).
* $P(F \mid \bar{B}) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$ (if not bribed, the detainee is freed only on an even roll $\{2, 4, 6\}$).

We wish to calculate $P(B \mid F)$:
$$P(B \mid F) = \frac{P(F \mid B)P(B)}{P(F \mid B)P(B) + P(F \mid \bar{B})P(\bar{B})}$$

Substituting the known values:
$$P(B \mid F) = \frac{1 \cdot q}{1 \cdot q + \frac{1}{2}(1-q)} = \frac{q}{q + \frac{1-q}{2}}$$

Multiplying the numerator and denominator by 2:
$$P(B \mid F) = \frac{2q}{q + 1}$$
