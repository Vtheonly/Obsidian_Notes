# 3.7. Comprehensive Exercise - The Worker Transit Problem

### 1. Problem Statement
A worker travels to work every morning using either their car ($v$) or the train ($t$). Their choice of transportation is modeled as follows:
* **Rule 1:** The worker never takes the train two days in a row.
* **Rule 2:** If they take their car on a given day, they are equally likely to take their car or the train the following day.

---

### 2. State Space and the Markov Property

#### Identifying the State Space
The worker has two options for transit each day. The state space of the system is:
$$S = \{t, \quad v\}$$
where $t$ represents taking the train and $v$ represents taking the car.

#### Verifying the Markov Property
The worker's choice of transportation tomorrow depends only on their choice of transportation today, and is independent of what they chose on any previous days. Because the future state depends only on the present state, the process satisfies the Markov property and can be modeled as a Discrete-Time Markov Chain.

---

### 3. Constructing the Transition Matrix ($P$)
We define our states in the order $(t, v)$. Now we determine the transition probabilities:

* **From State $t$ (Train):**
  Rule 1 states that the worker never takes the train two days in a row. Therefore, they must take their car the following day:
  $$P_{t \to t} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad P_{t \to v} = 1$$
* **From State $v$ (Car):**
  Rule 2 states that if they take their car today, they are equally likely to take their car or the train tomorrow:
  $$P_{v \to t} = \frac{1}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad P_{v \to v} = \frac{1}{2}$$

This gives us the transition matrix:
$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix}$$

#### Verifying if $P$ is Stochastic and Regular
* **Stochastic Check:**
  * Row 1 sum: $0 + 1 = 1$.
  * Row 2 sum: $1/2 + 1/2 = 1$.
  Since both rows sum to 1 and all elements are non-negative, the matrix is stochastic.
* **Regularity Check:**
  We calculate the second power of the matrix:
  $$P^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0(0) + 1(1/2) & 0(1) + 1(1/2) \\ (1/2)(0) + (1/2)(1/2) & (1/2)(1) + (1/2)(1/2) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 1/4 & 3/4 \end{pmatrix}$$
  Since all elements in $P^2$ are strictly positive ($> 0$), the transition matrix $P$ is regular.

---

### 4. Initial Distribution ($p^{(0)}$)
Suppose that on their first day of work, the worker decides their transit by rolling a fair six-sided die. They will take their car if and only if they roll a $6$.

* **Probability of taking the car ($v$):**
  $$P(\text{rolling a 6}) = \frac{1}{6}$$
* **Probability of taking the train ($t$):**
  $$P(\text{rolling a 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5}) = \frac{5}{6}$$

This gives us our initial probability vector:
$$p^{(0)} = \left(\frac{5}{6}, \quad \frac{1}{6}\right) \approx (0.8333, \quad 0.1667)$$

---

### 5. Calculating the State Distribution after 4 Steps

#### Calculating $P^4$
We can find the 4-step transition matrix by squaring $P^2$:
$$P^4 = P^2 \cdot P^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 1/4 & 3/4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 1/4 & 3/4 \end{pmatrix}$$

Multiplying the matrices:
$$P^4 = \begin{pmatrix} 
(1/2)(1/2) + (1/2)(1/4) & (1/2)(1/2) + (1/2)(3/4) \\ 
(1/4)(1/2) + (3/4)(1/4) & (1/4)(1/2) + (3/4)(3/4) 
\end{pmatrix}$$
$$P^4 = \begin{pmatrix} 
1/4 + 1/8 & 1/4 + 3/8 \\ 
1/8 + 3/16 & 1/8 + 9/16 
\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 
3/8 & 5/8 \\ 
5/16 & 11/16 
\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 
0.3750 & 0.6250 \\ 
0.3125 & 0.6875 
\end{pmatrix}$$

#### Calculating the state distribution $p^{(4)}$
Now we multiply the initial distribution by $P^4$:
$$p^{(4)} = p^{(0)} P^4 = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{5}{6} & \frac{1}{6} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 3/8 & 5/8 \\ 5/16 & 11/16 \end{pmatrix}$$

Multiplying the vector and matrix:
$$p^{(4)} = \left( \frac{5}{6} \cdot \frac{3}{8} + \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{5}{16}, \quad \frac{5}{6} \cdot \frac{5}{8} + \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{11}{16} \right)$$
$$p^{(4)} = \left( \frac{15}{48} + \frac{5}{96}, \quad \frac{25}{48} + \frac{11}{96} \right)$$
To add these fractions, we use a common denominator of 96:
$$p^{(4)} = \left( \frac{30 + 5}{96}, \quad \frac{50 + 11}{96} \right) = \left( \frac{35}{96}, \quad \frac{61}{96} \right) \approx (0.3646, \quad 0.6354)$$

This means that on the 5th day (after 4 transitions), the probability the worker takes the train is approximately $36.46\%$, and the probability they take their car is approximately $63.54\%$.

---

### 6. Long-Term Habits
Since the transition matrix $P$ is regular, Theorem 3.1 guarantees that the state distribution will converge to a unique stationary distribution $t$ over time, regardless of the initial starting state.

We calculated this stationary distribution in Example A:
$$t = \left(\frac{1}{3}, \quad \frac{2}{3}\right) \approx (0.3333, \quad 0.6667)$$

#### Conclusion and Interpretation
* In the long run, the worker will take the train on approximately $33.33\%$ of days and their car on $66.67\%$ of days.
* On their first day, the worker had a high probability of taking the train ($5/6 \approx 83.33\%$). However, because they never take the train two days in a row, this high probability quickly decays, and by the 5th day, the distribution ($36.46\%$ train, $63.54\%$ car) is already very close to the long-term average. This demonstrates how the initial starting state has less and less influence on the system over time.
