# 1.3. Sigma Algebras and Measurable Spaces

### 1. The Need for $\sigma$-Algebras
When dealing with infinite or uncountable sample spaces (such as the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$), it is mathematically impossible to consistently assign a probability to every single subset of $\Omega$ without running into logical contradictions (such as the Vitali sets or the Banach-Tarski paradox). 

To resolve this, we restrict our probability calculations to a specific family of subsets called a **$\sigma$-algebra** (or **tribu** in French). This family contains only the subsets of $\Omega$ to which we can assign a well-defined probability.

### 2. Formal Definition of a $\sigma$-Algebra
Let $\Omega$ be a sample space, and let $\mathcal{P}(\Omega)$ denote the power set of $\Omega$ (the set of all subsets of $\Omega$). A collection $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(\Omega)$ is called a **$\sigma$-algebra** on $\Omega$ if it satisfies the following three axioms:

1. **Contains the Sample Space:** The sample space itself belongs to the collection:
   $$\Omega \in \mathcal{A}$$
2. **Closed under Complementation:** If an event is in the collection, its complement is also in the collection:
   $$\text{If } A \in \mathcal{A}, \quad \text{then } \bar{A} \in \mathcal{A}$$
3. **Closed under Countable Unions:** If we have a countable sequence of events in the collection, their union is also in the collection:
   $$\text{If } (A_i)_{i \in \mathbb{N}} \in \mathcal{A}, \quad \text{then } \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i \in \mathcal{A}$$

A pair $(\Omega, \mathcal{A})$ consisting of a sample space and a valid $\sigma$-algebra is called a **measurable space** (or *espace probabilisable*).

---

### 3. Propositions and Proofs

#### Proposition 1.1: $\emptyset \in \mathcal{A}$
* **Proof:** By Axiom 1, we know that $\Omega \in \mathcal{A}$. By Axiom 2, the complement of any member of $\mathcal{A}$ must also belong to $\mathcal{A}$. The complement of the sample space is the empty set:
  $$\bar{\Omega} = \Omega \setminus \Omega = \emptyset$$
  Therefore, $\emptyset \in \mathcal{A}$.

#### Proposition 1.2: Countable Intersections
If $(A_i)_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$ is a countable family of events in $\mathcal{A}$, then:
$$\bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i \in \mathcal{A}$$
* **Proof:** Since $A_i \in \mathcal{A}$ for all $i \in \mathbb{N}$, Axiom 2 guarantees that their complements $\bar{A}_i$ are also in $\mathcal{A}$. By Axiom 3, the countable union of these complements is in $\mathcal{A}$:
  $$\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} \bar{A}_i \in \mathcal{A}$$
  Applying Axiom 2 again, the complement of this union must also be in $\mathcal{A}$:
  $$\overline{\left( \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} \bar{A}_i \right)} \in \mathcal{A}$$
  According to De Morgan's Laws:
  $$\overline{\left( \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} \bar{A}_i \right)} = \bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty} \overline{\bar{A}_i} = \bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i$$
  This proves that $\bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i \in \mathcal{A}$.

#### Proposition 1.3: Difference Operations
If $A, B \in \mathcal{A}$, then:
1. **Set Difference:** $A \setminus B \in \mathcal{A}$
2. **Symmetric Difference:** $A \Delta B \in \mathcal{A}$

* **Proof for Set Difference:**
  We can express the set difference using intersections and complements:
  $$A \setminus B = A \cap \bar{B}$$
  Since $B \in \mathcal{A}$, its complement $\bar{B}$ belongs to $\mathcal{A}$ (by complementation closure). Since $A \in \mathcal{A}$ and $\bar{B} \in \mathcal{A}$, their intersection $A \cap \bar{B}$ belongs to $\mathcal{A}$ (by intersection closure). Thus, $A \setminus B \in \mathcal{A}$.

* **Proof for Symmetric Difference:**
  The symmetric difference represents the event where either $A$ or $B$ occurs, but not both. It is defined as:
  $$A \Delta B = (A \setminus B) \cup (B \setminus A)$$
  We have already proved that $(A \setminus B) \in \mathcal{A}$ and $(B \setminus A) \in \mathcal{A}$. Since a $\sigma$-algebra is closed under unions, their union $(A \setminus B) \cup (B \setminus A)$ also belongs to $\mathcal{A}$. Thus, $A \Delta B \in \mathcal{A}$.
