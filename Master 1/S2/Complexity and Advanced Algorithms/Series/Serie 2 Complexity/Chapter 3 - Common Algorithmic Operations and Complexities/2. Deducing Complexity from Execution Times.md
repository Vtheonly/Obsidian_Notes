# 2. Deducing Complexity from Execution Times

In Exercise 6, we are given a table of execution times (in milliseconds) for three mysterious algorithms $A_1$, $A_2$, and $A_3$ as the input size $n$ increases. 

We must deduce their asymptotic complexity $O(?)$ by looking at the growth rate (witness values).

## Table Analysis
| $n$ | $A_1$ time | $A_2$ time | $A_3$ time |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 2 | 10 | 3 |
| 2 | 1 | 27 | 5 |
| 3 | 3 | 50 | 8 |
| 4 | 2 | 73 | 12 |
| 8 | 4 | 318 | 260 |
| 10| 4 | 493 | 1010 |
| 16| 5 | 3270| 65200|
| 17| 6 | 4083| 132000|

---

### Algorithm $A_1$: Extremely Slow Growth
Look at how $A_1$ reacts to massive changes in $n$:
* When $n=2$, time = $1$.
* When $n=16$, time = $5$.
As input size multiplied by 8 (from 2 to 16), the time only grew slightly. The relationship here is logarithmic. Notice that $\log_2(16) = 4$, which correlates tightly with the execution time staying in the $4-5$ range.
**Conclusion:** $A_1 \in O(\log n)$

### Algorithm $A_2$: Polynomial Growth
Let's analyze ratios for $A_2$. When $n$ doubles, how does the time multiply?
* $n=4 \to n=8$: time goes from $73 \to 318$. Ratio: $318 / 73 \approx 4.3$
* $n=8 \to n=16$: time goes from $318 \to 3270$. Ratio: $3270 / 318 \approx 10$
Let's check polynomial cubes ($n^3$):
* $10^3 = 1000$ (actual time is 493, half of $n^3$)
* $16^3 = 4096$ (actual time is 3270)
* $17^3 = 4913$ (actual time is 4083)
The execution time closely shadows $n^3$ (specifically, roughly $\approx 0.5 \times n^3$).
**Conclusion:** $A_2 \in O(n^3)$

### Algorithm $A_3$: Explosive / Exponential Growth
Notice what happens when $n$ increases just slightly at the end:
* $n=8 \to 260$
* $n=10 \to 1010$ (approx $2^{10} = 1024$)
* $n=16 \to 65200$ (approx $2^{16} = 65536$)
* $n=17 \to 132000$ (approx $2^{17} = 131072$)
The time values perfectly mirror powers of 2. The algorithm's time doubles every time you add a single item to the input ($n \to n+1$).
**Conclusion:** $A_3 \in O(2^n)$
