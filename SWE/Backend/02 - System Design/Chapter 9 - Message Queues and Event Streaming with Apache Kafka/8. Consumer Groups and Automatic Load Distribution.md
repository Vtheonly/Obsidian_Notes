# Consumer Groups and Automatic Load Distribution

## Overview

Consumer groups are Kafka's mechanism for scaling consumption and providing automatic load distribution across multiple consumer instances. When multiple consumers belong to the same consumer group, Kafka automatically divides the topic partitions among them, ensuring that each partition is consumed by exactly one consumer within the group. This enables horizontal scaling of consumption: as throughput requirements increase, you can add more consumer instances to the group, and Kafka will automatically rebalance the partition assignments. Consumer groups also provide the foundation for competing consumers pattern, publish-subscribe semantics, and fault-tolerant consumption with automatic failover.

## Key Concepts

### Group Coordination

Each consumer group has a designated broker called the group coordinator that manages the group's membership and partition assignments. When a consumer joins or leaves the group, the coordinator triggers a rebalance operation that reassigns partitions among the active consumers. The coordinator also tracks each consumer's heartbeat to detect failures: if a consumer stops sending heartbeats within the `session.timeout.ms` period, the coordinator considers it dead and triggers a rebalance to reassign its partitions to the remaining consumers. This automatic failure detection and recovery ensures that consumption continues even when individual consumer instances fail.

### Partition Assignment Strategies

Kafka supports multiple partition assignment strategies that determine how partitions are distributed among consumers in a group. The Range assignor divides partitions by topic, assigning contiguous ranges of partitions to each consumer. The RoundRobin assignor distributes partitions across all consumers regardless of topic, providing more even distribution. The Sticky assignor minimizes partition movement during rebalances by preserving as many existing assignments as possible. The CooperativeSticky assignor further reduces disruption by performing incremental rebalances that only move partitions that must change, rather than revoking all partitions from all consumers during every rebalance.

### Rebalancing Process

A rebalance occurs whenever the group membership changes: a new consumer joins, an existing consumer leaves, or a consumer is deemed dead by the coordinator. During an eager rebalance, all consumers first revoke their assigned partitions, then receive their new assignments. This causes a brief pause in consumption as all consumers stop processing, revoke their partitions, and wait for new assignments. Cooperative rebalancing reduces this disruption by only moving the partitions that need to be reassigned, allowing consumers that are not affected by the rebalance to continue processing without interruption. The choice of assignor and rebalance protocol has significant implications for consumption latency and stability.

### Scaling Consumption Horizontally

Consumer groups enable horizontal scaling of consumption by allowing you to add more consumer instances to handle increased load. If a topic has six partitions and you start with two consumers in a group, each consumer handles three partitions. If throughput requirements double, you can add two more consumers, and each consumer will handle roughly one or two partitions. The maximum number of useful consumers in a group equals the number of partitions: adding more consumers beyond the partition count results in idle consumers. This means the partition count of a topic sets an upper bound on consumption parallelism for that topic.

### Consumer Group as a Logical Subscriber

Each consumer group acts as a logical subscriber to a topic. Different consumer groups consume the same topic independently, each maintaining its own offsets and processing events at its own pace. This enables the publish-subscribe pattern at scale: one topic can feed multiple independent applications simultaneously. For example, an "orders" topic might be consumed by a Payment Service group, an Inventory Service group, a Notification Service group, and an Analytics Pipeline group. Each group processes the same events but for different purposes, and the failure or slowdown of one group does not affect the others.

### Offset Commits per Group

Each consumer group maintains its own committed offsets for each partition it consumes. This means that if one consumer group has processed up to offset 1000 and another group has only processed up to offset 500, both positions are independently tracked. When a consumer in a group crashes and its partitions are reassigned to another consumer, the new consumer resumes from the last committed offset for its group. This independent offset tracking is what enables multiple consumer groups to process the same topic at different rates without interfering with each other.

## Practical Applications

Consider a food delivery application where the "new_orders" topic is consumed by a consumer group of Kitchen Service instances. During lunch rush, the kitchen receives hundreds of orders per minute, so you deploy five Kitchen Service instances, each consuming from a different partition. Kafka distributes the load automatically among these five consumers. When the rush subsides and you scale down to two instances, Kafka rebalances the partitions to the remaining consumers. In a ride-sharing platform, driver location updates are consumed by a group of matching service instances that pair riders with drivers. Each instance handles a subset of driver partitions, and Kafka automatically redistributes partitions if any instance fails.
