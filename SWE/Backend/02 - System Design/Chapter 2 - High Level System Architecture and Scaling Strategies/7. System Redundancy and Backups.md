# System Redundancy and Backups

## Overview

Redundancy and backups are the primary mechanisms for protecting data and ensuring service continuity in the face of hardware failures, software bugs, natural disasters, and human error. Redundancy ensures that a system can continue operating when a component fails, while backups ensure that data can be recovered if it is lost or corrupted. Together, they form the safety net that allows production systems to achieve high availability and durability. Understanding the different redundancy models, backup strategies, and recovery metrics is essential for designing systems that can withstand and recover from failures without data loss or extended downtime.

## Key Concepts

### Active-Active vs Active-Passive

In an active-active redundancy model, multiple instances of a service are running simultaneously and actively serving traffic. If one instance fails, the load balancer automatically routes traffic to the remaining instances. This model provides the best performance because all instances contribute to handling load, and failover is essentially instantaneous since the surviving instances are already running and serving requests. In an active-passive model, one instance is actively serving traffic while the other stands by in a standby state, ready to take over if the active instance fails. Failover in active-passive requires detecting the failure and promoting the standby, which introduces a brief period of unavailability. Active-active is preferred for its seamless failover, but it requires careful coordination to ensure data consistency across active instances.

### Data Replication Strategies

Data replication involves copying data across multiple machines or locations to ensure durability and availability. Synchronous replication means that a write operation is not considered complete until it has been confirmed by all replicas. This ensures strong consistency but increases write latency because the operation must wait for the slowest replica. Asynchronous replication means that the primary acknowledges the write immediately and replicates it to other nodes in the background. This provides lower write latency but introduces the risk of data loss if the primary fails before the replication completes. Semi-synchronous replication is a compromise where the primary waits for at least one replica to confirm the write before acknowledging it. The choice of replication strategy depends on the consistency requirements of the application and the acceptable tradeoff between latency and durability.

### Backup Types: Full, Incremental, and Differential

Full backups capture a complete copy of all data. They are simple to restore from but time-consuming and storage-intensive to create. Incremental backups capture only the data that has changed since the last backup (whether full or incremental). They are fast to create and use minimal storage, but restoring from incremental backups requires applying the full backup and then each incremental backup in sequence, which is time-consuming and error-prone. Differential backups capture all data that has changed since the last full backup. They offer a middle ground: faster to create than full backups and simpler to restore than incremental backups (only the full backup plus the latest differential is needed). Most production systems use a combination: weekly full backups with daily incremental or differential backups.

### Recovery Point Objective (RPO)

The Recovery Point Objective (RPO) defines the maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time. An RPO of 1 hour means the system can tolerate losing up to 1 hour of data in a disaster. RPO determines how frequently backups or replication must occur. If the RPO is 5 minutes, backups must be taken at least every 5 minutes, or synchronous replication must be used to ensure no data is lost. RPO is a business decision that balances the cost of more frequent backups or replication against the cost of potential data loss. For financial systems, the RPO is typically near zero, requiring synchronous replication. For analytics systems, an RPO of several hours may be acceptable.

### Recovery Time Objective (RTO)

The Recovery Time Objective (RTO) defines the maximum acceptable time to restore service after a failure. An RTO of 15 minutes means the system must be back online within 15 minutes of a failure. RTO determines the speed and automation of recovery procedures. A low RTO requires automated failover, pre-provisioned standby infrastructure, and well-rehearsed recovery procedures. A higher RTO allows for manual intervention and slower recovery processes. RTO and RPO together define the disaster recovery requirements of a system. Achieving low RTO and RPO typically requires significant investment in redundancy, automation, and operational readiness.

## Practical Applications

A large e-commerce platform might use active-active deployment across two availability zones with synchronous database replication, achieving an RPO of zero (no data loss) and an RTO of near zero (automatic failover). Daily incremental backups to an off-site location provide an additional safety net against catastrophic failures like data corruption or accidental deletion. The backup strategy is tested regularly through disaster recovery drills that simulate real failure scenarios. For a content delivery platform, an active-passive setup with asynchronous replication might be sufficient, with an RPO of a few seconds and an RTO of a few minutes, trading slightly higher data loss risk for lower infrastructure costs.
