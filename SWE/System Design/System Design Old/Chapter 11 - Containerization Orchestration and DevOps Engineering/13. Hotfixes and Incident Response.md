# Hotfixes and Incident Response

## Overview

Hotfixes and incident response are the emergency procedures of software operations -- the processes that teams follow when something goes wrong in production and users are affected. An incident is any event that degrades the user experience or threatens system reliability, ranging from a minor increase in error rates to a complete service outage. Incident response defines how teams detect, triage, mitigate, and resolve incidents, while hotfixes are emergency code changes deployed outside the normal release cycle to address critical production issues. Effective incident response requires preparation (runbooks, on-call rotations, communication protocols), practice (incident drills, game days), and a culture of learning (blameless post-mortems). The goal is not to prevent all incidents -- an impossible task in complex systems -- but to minimize their impact and learn from them continuously.

## Key Concepts

### Incident Severity and Classification

Incidents are classified by severity to determine the appropriate level of response. A Severity 1 (SEV1) incident typically indicates a complete service outage affecting all users, requiring an all-hands response. A SEV2 incident indicates a significant degradation affecting a large subset of users. SEV3 and SEV4 incidents represent progressively less urgent issues. The classification determines who is paged, how quickly they are expected to respond, and the communication cadence. Clear severity criteria prevent under-response (treating a major outage as a minor issue) and over-response (treating a minor blip as a crisis). Severity should be based on user impact, not on the technical complexity of the underlying issue.

### The Incident Response Lifecycle

Incident response follows a structured lifecycle: detection, triage, mitigation, resolution, and post-mortem. Detection involves identifying that an incident is occurring, typically through automated alerts from monitoring systems. Triage involves assessing the severity, identifying the affected components, and assigning an incident commander to coordinate the response. Mitigation focuses on restoring service as quickly as possible, even if the root cause has not been identified -- common mitigation strategies include rolling back a deployment, failing over to a standby system, or disabling a problematic feature flag. Resolution involves identifying and fixing the root cause. The post-mortem documents what happened, why it happened, and what actions will be taken to prevent recurrence.

### Hotfix Procedures

A hotfix is an emergency code change that bypasses the normal release process to address a critical production issue. Hotfixes follow an abbreviated workflow: the change is made, reviewed (quickly but thoroughly), tested (at minimum with smoke tests and the failing test case), and deployed directly to production. The key risks of hotfixes are that the abbreviated process may introduce new bugs, and the urgency may lead to shortcuts in testing. To mitigate these risks, hotfixes should be as small and targeted as possible, the normal CI/CD pipeline should still be used (just with a faster cadence), and the change should be reverted if it does not resolve the issue. All hotfixes should be followed by a post-mortem to understand why the normal process was insufficient.

### Communication During Incidents

Effective communication is as important as technical resolution during incidents. An incident commander is responsible for coordinating the response, ensuring that the right people are involved, and communicating status updates to stakeholders. A dedicated incident channel (typically a Slack channel) centralizes all communication, preventing the fragmentation that occurs when updates are spread across multiple channels. Status pages provide external communication to users, managing expectations and reducing support burden. Regular status updates -- even if the update is "we are still investigating" -- prevent information vacuums that lead to speculation and anxiety. After the incident, a summary of the timeline, impact, and resolution should be published to the broader organization.

### Blameless Post-Mortems

The post-mortem is the most important part of the incident lifecycle, because it is where the organization learns and improves. A blameless post-mortem focuses on systemic causes rather than individual mistakes: instead of asking "who broke the system?" it asks "what conditions made it possible for the system to break?" Common systemic causes include insufficient monitoring (the issue was not detected quickly enough), missing automated safeguards (a human had to intervene manually), inadequate testing (the bug was not caught before deployment), and organizational pressure to prioritize speed over safety. Action items from the post-mortem should be tracked and prioritized, ensuring that the lessons learned translate into concrete improvements.

## Practical Applications

The high-scale benchmarking scenario provides a vivid illustration of why incident response procedures matter. When running infrastructure costing $30 per hour, a mistake "would cost your company tens of thousands of dollars." The presenter noted that "a mistake here is not a bug" -- at that scale, even a suboptimal algorithmic choice (Big-O of n instead of log n) can be catastrophic. The emphasis on resource monitoring, having separate terminals for monitoring and operations, and the careful, step-by-step approach to each test reflects the same discipline required in incident response. In production, the equivalent discipline includes having runbooks for common failure modes, automated rollback mechanisms, and clear communication protocols that prevent a bad situation from getting worse.
