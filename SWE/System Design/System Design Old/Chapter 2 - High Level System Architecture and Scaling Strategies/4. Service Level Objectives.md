# Service Level Objectives

## Overview

Service Level Objectives (SLOs) are the internal targets that define the expected level of service for a system. An SLO specifies a measurable characteristic of the service, such as latency, availability, or throughput, along with a target value that the team commits to achieving. SLOs are the foundation of reliability engineering: they provide a clear, quantitative definition of what "good enough" means, enabling teams to make informed tradeoffs between feature development and reliability work. Without SLOs, teams have no objective way to determine whether the system is healthy, whether incidents are serious, or where to invest engineering effort.

## Key Concepts

### SLO Definition

An SLO is a specific, measurable goal for a service's behavior. A well-formed SLO consists of a Service Level Indicator (SLI), a target, and a time window. An SLI is the metric being measured, such as the percentage of requests that succeed, the median response latency, or the throughput in requests per second. The target is the value the team aims to achieve, such as 99.9% successful requests or a P99 latency below 500 milliseconds. The time window defines the period over which the SLO is evaluated, such as a rolling 30-day window. For example, a complete SLO might be: "99.9% of HTTP requests to the /api/v1/products endpoint will return a 200 status code, measured over a rolling 30-day window."

### How to Set SLOs

Setting SLOs requires understanding both user expectations and system capabilities. The process begins by identifying the critical user journeys and the metrics that matter most for those journeys. For a web application, this might include page load time, API response latency, and error rates. For a batch processing system, it might include job completion time and data freshness. The initial SLO targets should be based on current performance: measure what the system currently delivers and set the SLO slightly above that baseline, providing room for improvement without being unrealistic. SLOs should be ambitious but achievable, and they should be refined over time based on operational experience and user feedback.

### Measuring Against SLOs

Once SLOs are defined, the team must continuously measure actual performance against the targets. This requires robust monitoring and alerting infrastructure that tracks the relevant SLIs and compares them to the SLO targets in real time. Dashboards should display current SLO status, showing how much of the error budget has been consumed and how much remains. When performance approaches or breaches an SLO threshold, alerts should notify the on-call team so they can investigate and remediate. The key principle is that SLOs should drive operational priorities: when the system is within SLO, the team can focus on feature development; when the system is at risk of breaching SLO, reliability work takes precedence.

### Error Budgets

An error budget is the inverse of an SLO: if the SLO is 99.9% availability, the error budget is 0.1% of downtime allowed within the measurement window. Over a 30-day month, a 0.1% error budget translates to approximately 43.2 minutes of allowed downtime. Error budgets provide a framework for balancing reliability and feature velocity. As long as the error budget has not been exhausted, the team can deploy new features, make architectural changes, and take calculated risks. When the error budget is nearly exhausted, the team must focus on reliability: fixing bugs, improving monitoring, adding redundancy, and reducing the risk of further incidents. This framework prevents the common conflict between development teams (who want to ship features) and operations teams (who want stability) by providing a shared, quantitative basis for prioritization.

### Relationship to SLAs

SLOs are closely related to Service Level Agreements (SLAs), but they serve different purposes. An SLO is an internal target that the team strives to meet; it is a tool for engineering and operational decision-making. An SLA is a contractual commitment to customers that includes financial or other penalties if the agreed-upon service levels are not met. SLOs are typically stricter than SLAs because the team wants a safety margin between what they aim for and what they are contractually obligated to provide. If the SLO is 99.95% availability, the SLA might be 99.9%, giving the team a 0.05% buffer before they incur contractual penalties.

## Practical Applications

A social media platform might set SLOs for different tiers of service: the core feed-loading API might have an SLO of 99.99% availability and P99 latency under 200 milliseconds, while the notification service might have a less stringent SLO of 99.9% availability. This differentiation ensures that engineering effort is focused where it matters most to users. When an incident causes the feed API to breach its SLO, the error budget framework dictates that the team prioritizes fixing the feed over other work. By making reliability goals explicit and quantitative, SLOs transform reliability from a vague aspiration into a measurable, manageable engineering practice.
