# Service Level Agreements

## Overview

A Service Level Agreement (SLA) is a formal, contractual commitment between a service provider and its customers that defines the minimum level of service the provider guarantees to deliver. Unlike Service Level Objectives (SLOs), which are internal goals, SLAs carry legal and financial consequences if the agreed-upon service levels are not met. SLAs are a cornerstone of commercial relationships in the cloud computing industry, where enterprises rely on third-party providers for critical infrastructure. Understanding SLAs is essential for system designers because they define the reliability commitments that the architecture must support and the financial risks associated with service failures.

## Key Concepts

### SLA Definition

An SLA is a document or clause in a contract that specifies the minimum acceptable level of service, typically expressed as a percentage of availability or a maximum response time, along with the remedies available to the customer if the provider fails to meet those commitments. The SLA defines what is being measured, how it is measured, the target values, the measurement period, and the consequences of breach. For example, an SLA might state: "The service will be available 99.99% of the time, measured monthly. If availability falls below this threshold, the customer is entitled to a service credit equal to 10% of their monthly bill for each 0.01% below the target."

### Contractual Nature

The key distinction between an SLA and an SLO is the contractual nature of the SLA. An SLO is an internal target that guides engineering decisions; an SLA is a legally binding commitment that carries financial consequences. When a company signs an SLA with a cloud provider, both parties agree to specific terms, and the provider is obligated to meet those terms or provide compensation. This contractual nature means that SLAs are typically set conservatively, with a safety margin between the SLA target and the internal SLO. If the internal SLO is 99.95% availability, the SLA might promise only 99.9%, giving the provider a buffer before incurring financial penalties.

### Penalties for Violation

SLA violations typically result in service credits, which are discounts or refunds applied to the customer's bill. The penalty structure is usually tiered: a minor breach might result in a 10% credit, while a major breach might result in a 25% or higher credit. For example, AWS offers service credits for EC2 if monthly uptime falls below 99.99%, with a 10% credit for 99.0-99.99% uptime and a 25% credit for below 99.0% uptime. It is important to note that service credits are the exclusive remedy for SLA violations; customers cannot sue for additional damages beyond the credit amount. This means that the financial risk to the provider is capped and predictable, while the actual cost to the customer of a service outage (lost revenue, reputational damage) may far exceed the credit amount.

### Difference Between SLA and SLO

SLOs and SLAs serve complementary but distinct purposes. An SLO is an internal reliability target that drives engineering and operational decisions. It is typically more aggressive than the SLA to provide a safety margin. An SLA is an external commitment that defines the minimum acceptable service level and the consequences of falling short. Not all services have SLAs (internal services typically do not), but every service that users depend on should have SLOs. A well-run service has SLOs that are stricter than its SLAs, giving the team early warning when performance is degrading before it reaches the contractual threshold. The gap between the SLO and SLA is the team's operational buffer.

### Examples from Cloud Providers

Major cloud providers publish SLAs for their services. AWS guarantees 99.99% availability for EC2 in a single region, 99.995% availability for S3, and 99.99% availability for DynamoDB. Google Cloud offers 99.95% availability for Compute Engine and 99.95% for Cloud SQL. Azure provides 99.99% availability for Virtual Machines with availability sets. These SLAs vary by service, region, and configuration. Multi-region deployments often have higher SLA targets than single-region deployments. Understanding these SLAs is critical when designing systems that must meet specific availability requirements, because the SLAs of the underlying services impose an upper bound on the availability that the overall system can guarantee.

## Practical Applications

When designing a system that must guarantee 99.99% availability to end users, the engineer must ensure that every component in the critical path has an SLA that supports this target. If the application runs on a cloud service with a 99.9% SLA, the system cannot guarantee better than 99.9% overall availability without redundancy across providers or regions. This is why multi-region architectures and multi-cloud strategies are used for the most demanding availability requirements. Understanding SLAs also helps engineers calculate the financial risk of downtime: if an SLA breach costs the company a $10,000 service credit but the actual business impact of an hour of downtime is $100,000 in lost revenue, the SLA credit provides inadequate compensation, and additional redundancy investments are justified.
