# 6.3 Weight Initialization in CNNs

Weight initialization is one of the most underappreciated yet critically important aspects of training deep CNNs. The choice of initial values for the filter weights can mean the difference between a network that trains successfully and one that never learns anything at all. Despite its importance, many practitioners simply use the default initialization provided by their framework without understanding why it works or what would happen without it.

### The Catastrophe of Zero Initialization

You cannot initialize raw CNN architecture weights statically to all explicit absolute zeros; otherwise, every single active filter natively computes the identical exact same gradients internally and therefore learns the identical exact same topological features. This is referred to fundamentally as a **Symmetry Breaking** failure.

To understand why this is catastrophic, consider what happens during the forward pass: if all filters in a layer are initialized to zeros, they will all produce the exact same output values (all zeros before bias, all bias values after bias). During backpropagation, since all filters produced the same output, they will all receive the same gradient. And since they all have the same current weight values (zero) and receive the same gradient, they will all be updated by the same amount. After the update, they will still all have identical weight values. This symmetry persists forever — no matter how many training iterations you run, all filters will remain identical, effectively making the layer no more powerful than a single filter.

This is why random initialization is essential: it breaks the symmetry by giving each filter a different starting point, which leads to different outputs, different gradients, and ultimately different learned features.

### The Failure of Standard Random Initialization

Furthermore, a standard Gaussian random uniform initialization actively fails dynamically in deep CNN architectures because statistical mathematical variance destructively shrinks or violently explodes as matrix data continuously passes through 50+ sequentially connected active hierarchical layers. This is the **Vanishing or Exploding Gradients Problem**, and it is the primary reason why naive random initialization does not work for deep networks.

#### The Vanishing Gradient Problem
If weights are initialized from a distribution with too-small variance, the activations at each layer will have progressively smaller variance as data passes through the network. After 50 layers, the activations may become so small that they are effectively zero. During backpropagation, the gradients will also be extremely small (because the chain rule multiplies many small numbers together), making learning impossibly slow. The network appears to train, but the early layers never actually learn meaningful features.

#### The Exploding Gradient Problem
If weights are initialized from a distribution with too-large variance, the activations at each layer will have progressively larger variance. After 50 layers, the activations may overflow the numerical representation (becoming NaN or Infinity), causing the entire training process to crash. Even if overflow does not occur, the gradients will be extremely large, causing the optimizer to take massive, unstable steps that prevent convergence.

### The Mathematical Solution: Kaiming (He) Initialization

Developed specifically for modern CNN architectures using ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) non-linear activations, Kaiming Initialization (also known as He Initialization) solves the vanishing/exploding gradient problem by carefully controlling the variance of the initialization distribution.

#### How It Operates
It initializes numerical parameter weights randomly, but strictly scales the variance of the randomness based on the size of the filter ($K \times K \times C_{in}$). This mathematically ensures that the variance of the feature maps remains perfectly stable from Layer 1 all the way to Layer 100.

The initialization distribution is set so that:

$$ \text{Var}(W) = \frac{2}{K \times K \times C_{in}} $$

Where $K$ is the kernel size and $C_{in}$ is the number of input channels (also called the "fan-in"). The factor of 2 accounts for the fact that ReLU zeroes out approximately half of the activations, effectively halving the variance at each layer. Without this factor of 2, the variance would halve at each ReLU, leading to vanishing activations over many layers.

#### Why This Specific Variance?
The mathematical derivation comes from analyzing the variance of the output of a convolutional layer. If the input has variance $\sigma_x^2$ and the weights have variance $\sigma_w^2$, then the output variance (before ReLU) is approximately:

$$ \sigma_y^2 = K \times K \times C_{in} \times \sigma_w^2 \times \sigma_x^2 $$

For the variance to remain stable across layers (i.e., $\sigma_y^2 = \sigma_x^2$), we need:

$$ K \times K \times C_{in} \times \sigma_w^2 = 1 $$

$$ \sigma_w^2 = \frac{1}{K \times K \times C_{in}} $$

But after ReLU (which zeros out approximately half the values), we need to compensate by multiplying by 2:

$$ \sigma_w^2 = \frac{2}{K \times K \times C_{in}} $$

This ensures that the variance of the feature maps remains approximately constant throughout the entire depth of the network.

### Comparison of Initialization Methods

| Method | Best For | Key Formula | Key Insight |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Kaiming (He) Normal** | ReLU activations | $\text{Var}(W) = \frac{2}{fan_{in}}$ | Compensates for ReLU zeroing half the values |
| **Kaiming (He) Uniform** | ReLU activations | $W \sim U(-\sqrt{6/fan_{in}}, \sqrt{6/fan_{in}})$ | Uniform distribution version of He init |
| **Xavier (Glorot) Normal** | Sigmoid / Tanh activations | $\text{Var}(W) = \frac{2}{fan_{in} + fan_{out}}$ | Balances variance preservation in both directions |
| **LeCun Normal** | SELU activations | $\text{Var}(W) = \frac{1}{fan_{in}}$ | Designed for self-normalizing networks |
| **Random Normal (naive)** | Never | $W \sim N(0, 1)$ | Causes vanishing/exploding in deep networks |
| **Zeros** | Never | $W = 0$ | Symmetry breaking failure |

Where $fan_{in} = K \times K \times C_{in}$ (number of input connections per neuron) and $fan_{out} = K \times K \times C_{out}$ (number of output connections per neuron).

### Practical Implementation in PyTorch

In PyTorch, `torch.nn.Conv2d` uses **Kaiming uniform initialization** by default. This means the weights are initialized from a uniform distribution $U(-a, a)$ where $a = \sqrt{6 / ((1 + a^2) \times fan_{in})}$ and $a^2$ is the negative slope of the ReLU (default 0 for standard ReLU). The bias is initialized from a uniform distribution $U(-1/\sqrt{fan_{in}}, 1/\sqrt{fan_{in}})$.

You generally don't need to change this default unless you're using non-ReLU activations (like sigmoid or tanh), in which case Xavier initialization is typically preferred because it accounts for the different statistical properties of those activation functions.

> [!tip] Practical Takeaway
> If you are building a standard CNN with ReLU activations, **use the default Kaiming initialization** provided by your framework. Do not initialize weights to zeros, ones, or standard normal values. If you are using non-ReLU activations, switch to Xavier initialization. The correct initialization can mean the difference between a network that converges in 10 epochs and one that never converges at all.
