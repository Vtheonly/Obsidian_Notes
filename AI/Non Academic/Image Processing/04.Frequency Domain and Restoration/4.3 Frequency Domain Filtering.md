# 4.3 Frequency Domain Filtering

## 1. The Fundamental Steps
To filter an image in the frequency domain, we follow this rigorous pipeline:
1.  **Pre-processing:** Pad the image with zeros to avoid "wrap-around" error.
2.  **Forward Transform:** Compute $F(u, v)$ using the FFT.
3.  **Shift:** Move low frequencies to the center.
4.  **Filtering:** Multiply $F(u, v)$ by a filter mask $H(u, v)$.
    $$ G(u, v) = H(u, v) \cdot F(u, v) $$
5.  **Inverse Shift:** Move frequencies back to corners.
6.  **Inverse Transform:** Compute the Inverse FFT to get back to pixels.
7.  **Post-processing:** Crop the image back to original size.

## 2. Low-Pass Filters (Smoothing)
These masks allow low frequencies to pass (value 1) and block high frequencies (value 0).

*   **Ideal LPF:** A perfect circle of 1s in a field of 0s.
    *   *Problem:* Causes **Ringing Artifacts** (ghost-like ripples around edges) because of the sharp "cliff" in the mask.
*   **Butterworth LPF:** A smooth transition. You can control the "steepness" with the order $n$. It is a great middle ground.
*   **Gaussian LPF:** A Bell Curve mask.
    *   *Benefit:* It is the only filter that **never produces ringing**. The result is a perfectly smooth, natural blur.

## 3. High-Pass Filters (Sharpening/Edges)
These block low frequencies (center of mask is 0) and allow high frequencies to pass.
*   **Result:** The "background" disappears, leaving only edges and fine details. It looks like a glowing outline of the image.

## 4. Band-Reject and Notch Filters
*   **Band-Reject:** Blocks a specific "ring" of frequencies.
*   **Notch Filter:** Blocks a specific "dot" (coordinate) in the frequency spectrum.
*   **Killer Use Case:** If an image has a pattern of diagonal lines across it (periodic noise), that noise will appear as a pair of bright dots in the spectrum. By placing a "Notch" (a zero) exactly on those dots, the lines **completely vanish** from the image without blurring the rest of the picture.
