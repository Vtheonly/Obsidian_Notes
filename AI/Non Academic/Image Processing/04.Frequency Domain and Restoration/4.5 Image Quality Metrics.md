# 4.5 Image Quality Metrics

How do we prove that "Filter A" is better than "Filter B"? We use mathematical metrics to compare the restored image ($\hat{f}$) to the original ground truth ($f$).

## 1. Mean Squared Error (MSE)
The average of the square of the errors between pixels.
$$ \text{MSE} = \frac{1}{M \times N} \sum_{x=0}^{M-1} \sum_{y=0}^{N-1} [f(x, y) - \hat{f}(x, y)]^2 $$
*   **Lower is better.** An MSE of 0 means the images are identical.

## 2. Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR)
Since image pixels usually go up to 255, we express the error as a ratio against the maximum possible signal.
$$ \text{PSNR} = 10 \cdot \log_{10} \left( \frac{MAX_I^2}{\text{MSE}} \right) $$
*   **Higher is better.** (Usually measured in Decibels, dB).
*   Typical values for good quality are between 30 and 50 dB.
*   *Why use it?* It is logarithmic, which matches human perception better than MSE.

## 3. Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)
**The Problem with MSE/PSNR:** You can take an image, shift it by 1 pixel to the left, and the MSE will be huge (because every pixel is compared to its neighbor). However, to a human, the image is identical.

**SSIM** is a more advanced metric that compares three aspects of images:
1.  **Luminance** (Brightness)
2.  **Contrast**
3.  **Structure** (Correlations between pixels)

*   **Range:** -1 to 1.
*   **1.0** means the images are structurally identical. It is widely considered the most "accurate" metric for how humans perceive image quality.
