# 4.2 The Fourier Transform (DFT and FFT)

To process images on a computer, we use the **Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)**.

## 1. The 1D DFT (The Foundation)
For a sequence of $N$ samples $f(x)$, the DFT $F(u)$ is defined as:
$$ F(u) = \sum_{x=0}^{N-1} f(x) e^{-j 2 \pi u x / N} $$
Using **Euler's Formula** ($e^{-j\theta} = \cos \theta - j \sin \theta$), this becomes:
$$ F(u) = \sum_{x=0}^{N-1} f(x) \left[ \cos(2\pi ux/N) - j \sin(2\pi ux/N) \right] $$
This formula tells us how much of a specific frequency $u$ exists in the signal $f(x)$.

## 2. The 2D DFT (The Image Version)
Since an image is a 2D matrix $f(x, y)$ of size $M \times N$, we apply the transform in two dimensions:
$$ F(u, v) = \sum_{x=0}^{M-1} \sum_{y=0}^{N-1} f(x, y) e^{-j 2 \pi (ux/M + vy/N)} $$
*   $u, v$ are the frequency variables.
*   $x, y$ are the spatial variables (pixels).

## 3. Components of the Result: Magnitude and Phase
The output of a Fourier Transform is a matrix of **Complex Numbers** ($a + bj$). To see it, we must split it into two parts:

### A. The Magnitude Spectrum (The "What")
$$ |F(u, v)| = \sqrt{R(u, v)^2 + I(u, v)^2} $$
The spectrum shows the **strength** of each frequency. 
*   **Visualizing:** Because the value at $(0,0)$ (the DC component or average brightness) is usually thousands of times larger than the others, we always apply a **Log Transform** (see Note 2.2) to see the spectrum: $D(u, v) = \log(1 + |F(u, v)|)$.

### B. The Phase Angle (The "Where")
$$ \phi(u, v) = \arctan\left( \frac{I(u, v)}{R(u, v)} \right) $$
The phase stores the **location** of the frequencies. 
*   **Crucial Insight:** While the spectrum is prettier to look at, the **Phase contains the most information** about the structure of the image. If you reconstruct an image using the magnitude of "Image A" and the phase of "Image B," the result will look like "Image B."

## 4. Centering the Transform (The FFT Shift)
In a raw DFT, the low frequencies (the most important ones) are located in the four corners of the matrix. For humans to analyze it, we perform a "shift" that moves the $(0,0)$ frequency to the **center** of the image.
*   In the spectrum, the center represents the background/lowest frequencies.
*   The edges represent the highest frequencies.
