# 5.2 Erosion and Dilation

These are the two fundamental operations. Every other morphological algorithm is built by combining these two.

## 1. Dilation ($\oplus$)
Dilation "grows" or "thickens" objects in a binary image.

### A. Mathematical Definition
$$ A \oplus B = \{z \mid (\hat{B})_z \cap A \neq \emptyset \} $$
In plain English: The dilation of $A$ by $B$ is the set of all points $z$ such that the reflected SE, when translated to $z$, **overlaps with at least one pixel** of $A$.

### B. Logical Process
1.  Place the origin of the SE on pixel $(x,y)$.
2.  Look at all image pixels covered by the SE.
3.  If **any** of those pixels are 1, the output at $(x,y)$ is 1.
4.  Otherwise, the output is 0.

### C. Applications
*   Bridging gaps: If a line is broken by a small 1-pixel gap, dilation will connect it.
*   Filling small holes: Pepper noise (black dots inside white objects) is filled.

## 2. Erosion ($\ominus$)
Erosion "shrinks" or "thins" objects. It is the mathematical dual of dilation.

### A. Mathematical Definition
$$ A \ominus B = \{z \mid (B)_z \subseteq A \} $$
In plain English: The erosion of $A$ by $B$ is the set of all points $z$ such that the SE, when translated to $z$, is **contained entirely** within $A$.

### B. Logical Process
1.  Place the origin of the SE on pixel $(x,y)$.
2.  Look at all image pixels covered by the SE.
3.  If **every single** covered pixel is 1, the output at $(x,y)$ is 1.
4.  If even one covered pixel is 0 (background), the output is 0.

### C. Applications
*   Eliminating noise: Small isolated white pixels (salt noise) disappear because the SE cannot "fit" inside them.
*   Separating objects: If two circles are touching by a thin neck, erosion will remove the neck, leaving two separate circles.

## 3. Duality
Erosion and Dilation are duals with respect to set complementation:
$$ (A \ominus B)^c = A^c \oplus \hat{B} $$
*Meaning:* Eroding the foreground is the same as dilating the background.
