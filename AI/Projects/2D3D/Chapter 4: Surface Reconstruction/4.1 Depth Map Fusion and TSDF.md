# 4.1 Depth Map Fusion and TSDF

## The Core Concept
At this stage in the pipeline, you have generated dozens or hundreds of Dense Depth Maps (either using Classical MVS or Neural Networks like Depth Anything V3). You also have perfectly aligned Extrinsic matrices ($[R|t]$) for each camera.

If you simply convert every depth map into a 3D point cloud and merge them, the result is terrible. Because of sensor noise, neural estimation softening, and sub-millimeter inaccuracies in pose estimation, the point clouds will perfectly overlap *almost nowhere*. 

Rather than seeing a sharp wall, you will see a noisy, fuzzy "crust" of points several centimeters thick. **Depth Map Fusion** (such as the mathematical geometry filter proposed by Fuhrmann et al., or the TSDF) is the mathematical post-processing phase that merges this chaos into a single, cohesive average surface. 

> [!NOTE] 
> Depth Map Fusion is **not** a neural network. Even if an advanced Deep Learning MVS model predicted the depth, fusion is mandatory. Deep learning is the "guess," but fusion is the mathematically hard-coded geometric check that verifies the guess against overlapping views to discard hallucinated floating noise.

The globally dominant volumetric algorithm for this is the **Truncated Signed Distance Function (TSDF)** (Curless and Levoy, 1996).

---

## 1. How Volumetric TSDF Works

Instead of trying to merge millions of floating points, TSDF merges **continuous 3D space**. It tracks the "empty air" and the "solid matter" to mathematically define the boundary between them.

### Step-by-Step Mathematical Algorithm

1.  **The Voxel Universe:** The entirely bounding box of the virtual world is divided into tens of millions of tiny 3D cubes called "voxels" (e.g., $1cm^3$ resolution).
2.  **Signed Distance ($D$):** For every single voxel, the algorithm asks: *"How far away are you from the nearest solid surface according to Camera 1?"*
    *   **Positive Distance ($+D$):** The voxel is floating in empty air between the camera lens and the object.
    *   **Negative Distance ($-D$):** The voxel is buried deeply inside the solid object (behind the wall).
    *   **Zero Distance ($0$):** Exactly on the geometric surface itself.
3.  **Truncation:** We don't care about voxels located 5 meters up in empty space. Calculating that wastes RAM. We mathematically truncate (clamp) the values only to a narrow band around the surface (e.g., $\pm 4$ cm), ignoring everything else.

### The Fusion Formula
If 50 different cameras observe the exact same voxel $x$ from different angles, we mathematically average their measurements to filter out the noise:

$$D(x) = \frac{\sum W_i(x) \cdot d_i(x)}{\sum W_i(x)}$$

*   $d_i(x)$: The individual signed distance measured by camera $i$.
*   $W_i(x)$: The **Confidence Weight**. A camera shooting straight at a wall head-on gets a massive weight (it is highly reliable). A camera taking a severe grazing angle shot (where depth estimation mathematically fails) gets a near-zero calculation weight.

## 2. Advanced: Sparse Voxel Hashing (Scalable TSDF)

Creating a $1000 \times 1000 \times 1000$ float array in Python requires 4 Gigabytes of continuous RAM just to hold empty zeroes. Reconstructing an outdoor building this way will physically crash a supercomputer.

To solve this, modern engines (like Open3D's `ScalableTSDFVolume`) use **Sparse Voxel Hashing**. They do not maintain a massive 3D grid. They maintain a 1D Hash Map. They dynamically allocate memory in tiny $8 \times 8 \times 8$ voxel chunks *only* exactly where a depth map mathematically hits a solid physical surface. The empty air is never allocated to RAM, reducing memory consumption by $95\%$.

### Implementation Status 🛠️
* **Requires Training?** **No.** Pure volumetric calculus.
* **Solo Developer Feasibility:** **A RAM Nightmare from Scratch.** While writing the averaging equation for a small $128 \times 128$ NumPy grid takes 20 lines of Python, the resulting mesh will look like giant, blurry Minecraft blocks due to low resolution. To write a Sparse Voxel Hash Map requires insanely optimized C++ pointers. Treat this as a hyper-optimized black box and exclusively call Open3D's `ScalableTSDFVolume`.
