# 1.1 Camera Models and Pinhole Geometry

## The Core Concept
The foundational concept of any 2D → 3D reconstruction pipeline is understanding how the physical 3D world is captured onto a 2D sensor. To bridge this dimension gap, almost all Computer Vision algorithms rely on the **Pinhole Camera Model**.

### What is the Pinhole Camera Model?
In reality, cameras use complex compound lenses that bend light. In mathematics, we simplify this heavily. A pinhole camera is an ideal camera with no lens at all. Instead, it has a tiny aperture (the "pinhole") through which all light rays from the outside world must pass before hitting the image plane (the digital sensor or film).

Because light travels in perfectly straight lines, every point in the 3D world emits a ray that travels in a straight line, passes through the exact center of the pinhole (the **Optical Center** or **Camera Center**), and strikes the sensor.

---

## The Mathematical Formulation

### The Perspective Projection
Let's define our coordinate system:
* Let the Camera Center be the origin point $(0, 0, 0)$ in 3D space.
* Let the Z-axis point directly out into the world (the **Principal Axis** or Optical Axis).
* Let the image plane be situated at a distance $f$ (the **Focal Length**) in front of the camera center.

If we have a 3D point in the physical world $P = (X, Y, Z)$, where does it land on the 2D image plane as pixel $p = (x, y)$?

Using simple geometric similar triangles, the relationship is defined by:
$$ x = f \frac{X}{Z} $$
$$ y = f \frac{Y}{Z} $$

### Explanation of Variables
* $P(X,Y,Z)$: The true physical coordinates of the object relative to the camera lens.
* $Z$: The physical depth, or distance of the object straight out from the camera. Notice that we divide by $Z$. This mathematical division is what causes **Perspective Foreshortening** (objects further away appear smaller on the screen).
* $f$: **Focal length**. The distance between the pinhole and the sensor. Increasing $f$ acts as an optical zoom, scaling up both $x$ and $y$.
* $(x, y)$: The physical 2D location on the sensor plane.

---

## Intuition and Perspective Geometry

The equation $x = f \frac{X}{Z}$ reveals why a single photograph cannot be used to reconstruct 3D space. 

Notice that you only measure $x$ (the pixel on your screen). You don't know $X$ (the real height) or $Z$ (the real depth).
An object that is 2 meters tall and 10 meters away ($2/10 = 0.2$) produces the exact same pixel size on the sensor as an object that is 4 meters tall and 20 meters away ($4/20 = 0.2$). 

This is the **Scale Ambiguity** problem. All depth information is lost upon projection because a 3D point is compressed into a 2D ray. To retrieve the 3D structure, you must take pictures of the same object from different angles and triangulate the overlapping rays (See: [[1.4 Epipolar and Multi-View Geometry]]).

## Practical Engineering Limitations

### Radial and Tangential Distortion
The pinhole model is a perfect mathematical dream. Real cameras use glass lenses. Glass lenses bend light imperfectly, resulting in **Distortion**.

* **Radial Distortion:** Straight lines appear curved. This happens because light bending through the incredibly thick edges of a glass lens behaves differently than light passing through the center. (Commonly seen as the "Fish-eye" barrel effect or "Pincushion" effect).
* **Tangential Distortion:** Happens when the glass lens is not mounted perfectly parallel to the image sensor inside the camera body.

**How it connects to the project code:**
In a classic 3D pipeline (like the one utilizing COLMAP), you must mathematically "undistort" the images before trying to run geometry algorithms. If you assume the camera follows perfect pinhole math, but the lens actively warped the image, all triangulation lines will miss each other by dozens of pixels, and the reconstruction will fail completely.

### Implementation Status 
* **Requires Training?** **No.** This relies purely on classical Euclidean geometry.
* **Solo Developer Feasibility:** **Implementable from scratch**. The projection equations are a few lines of basic Python. However, calculating the actual distortion parameters of a physical lens requires running a Checkerboard Calibration algorithm (widely available in OpenCV).
