# 1.2 Intrinsic and Extrinsic Parameters

## The Core Concept
In 3D Computer Vision, a camera cannot just "take a picture." To map the digital pixels of an image back to the physical reality of the universe, the computer requires a rigorous mathematical definition of the camera's exact state. 

This mathematical definition is split into two massive overarching matrix categories:
1. **Intrinsic Parameters:** The internal, physical mechanics of the camera (Lens type, Sensor size).
2. **Extrinsic Parameters:** The external, physical location of the camera floating in the universe (Position, Rotation).

---

## 1. Intrinsic Parameters (The $K$ Matrix)

The intrinsic parameters define how the camera internally projects light onto its digital sensor. These numbers do not change when the camera moves around the room. They only change if you swap the lens or change the zoom.

### Mathematical Formulation
The Intrinsics are represented by a $3 \times 3$ matrix called the **Camera Calibration Matrix**, denoted as $K$:

$$
K = \begin{bmatrix}
f_x & s & c_x \\
0 & f_y & c_y \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

### Explanation of Variables
* $f_x, f_y$: **Focal Lengths**. While a physical lens has one focal length ($f$), digital sensors have rectangular pixels. $f_x$ and $f_y$ represent the focal length scaled by the physical width and height of a single pixel on the sensor. 
* $c_x, c_y$: **Principal Point (Optical Center)**. Ideally, the exact center of the digital image (e.g., pixel 960, 540 on a 1920x1080 image). Due to manufacturing flaws, the true optical center is often slightly offset.
* $s$: **Skew coefficient**. Modern cameras have perfectly rectangular pixels, so this is almost always explicitly set to 0.

**Intuition:** The $K$ matrix takes a point floating in the local space directly in front of the lens, and translates it into a 2D integer pixel coordinate $(u, v)$ on your computer monitor.

---

## 2. Extrinsic Parameters (The $[R|t]$ Matrix)

The extrinsic parameters define exactly where the camera is physically positioned in the 3D World Space when the photo was taken. This establishes the camera's **Six Degrees of Freedom (6DoF)**.

### Mathematical Formulation
The Extrinsics are combined into a $4 \times 4$ transformation matrix, often referred to as Pose or $[R|t]$:

$$
Extrinsic = \begin{bmatrix}
r_{11} & r_{12} & r_{13} & t_x \\
r_{21} & r_{22} & r_{23} & t_y \\
r_{31} & r_{32} & r_{33} & t_z \\
0 & 0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

### Explanation of Variables
* **$R$ (The $3 \times 3$ Rotation Matrix):** Represented by the $r$ grids. This defines the angular tilt of the camera. It consists of Roll (rotation around Z), Pitch (rotation around X), and Yaw (rotation around Y). It defines what direction the lens is pointing.
* **$t$ (The $3 \times 1$ Translation Vector):** Represented by $t_x, t_y, t_z$. This is the absolute physical coordinate $(X, Y, Z)$ of the camera's optical center relative to the origin of the World space $(0,0,0)$. 

**Intuition:** The Extrinsic matrix is a transport mechanism. It takes a 3D point sitting in the global world space, and mathematically teleports it into the local coordinate system of the camera (making the camera the center of the universe).

---

## Connecting to the Pipeline

To rebuild a real-world object (See: [[4.1 Depth Map Fusion and TSDF]]), the software must know both matrices perfectly. 
1. If the software has the wrong Intrinsic $K$ matrix, the reconstructed 3D model will look warped, stretched, or artificially elongated like looking through a funhouse mirror.
2. If the software calculates the wrong Extrinsic $[R|t]$ matrix, the camera is tracking incorrectly, and the geometry fused from frame 1 will physically miss the geometry fused from frame 2, creating massive blurs or split "ghosts" in the 3D model.

### Implementation Status 🛠️
* **Requires Training?** **No.** Pure linear algebra.
* **Solo Developer Feasibility:** **Uses Pre-Built Algorithms.** While you can write matrix multiplication from scratch easily, you should absolutely *never* attempt to calculate these matrices from raw images yourself. You must rely on advanced Structure-from-Motion software (like COLMAP) to deduce the Extrinsics, and OpenCV routines to deduce the Intrinsics.
