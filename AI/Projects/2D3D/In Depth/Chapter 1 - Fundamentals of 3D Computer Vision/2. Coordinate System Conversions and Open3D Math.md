# 2. Coordinate System Conversions and Open3D Math

While the theory of $p = K \cdot [R|t] \cdot P_{world}$ is elegant, deploying it in code requires strict attention to matrix inversions. This is a topic that consistently breaks pipelines.

## World-to-Camera vs Camera-to-World
*   **World-to-Camera (Extrinsic):** This takes a point in the real world and moves it into the camera's local space. This is what SfM algorithms like COLMAP output and what your `FrameData.extrinsic` matrix stores.
*   **Camera-to-World (Pose):** This takes a pixel from the camera and projects it out into the real world. This is what Point Cloud generators need.

> ** Essential Reminder:** 
> If your point cloud looks like a chaotic explosion instead of a room, you forgot to invert your matrix. The Camera Center in world coordinates is mathematically derived as: $C = -R^T \cdot t$. In a $4 \times 4$ matrix, this is simply the inverse of the Extrinsic matrix.
> 
> ```python
> # This property in your types.py does exactly this:
> @property
> def camera_center(self) -> np.ndarray:
>     """Extract the camera position in world coordinates."""
>     return np.linalg.inv(self.extrinsic)[:3, 3]
> ```

## The Depth Projection Formula
When your pipeline converts a depth map into a 3D Point Cloud (`depth_to_pointcloud`), it executes the projection equation in reverse. Given a pixel at coordinates $(u, v)$ and its measured depth $Z$, the 3D coordinates in the local camera space are:

$$X = \frac{(u - c_x) \cdot Z}{f_x}$$
$$Y = \frac{(v - c_y) \cdot Z}{f_y}$$

Once the local $(X, Y, Z)$ point is found, you multiply it by the inverse Extrinsic matrix (Camera-to-World) to place the point precisely in the global 3D scene.
