# 2. Epipolar Geometry and Constraints

How does the computer filter out bad feature matches? It uses a law of physics known as **Epipolar Geometry**.

Imagine a 3D point $X$. Camera 1 is at center $C_1$, and Camera 2 is at center $C_2$. 
The three points in space ($X, C_1, C_2$) form a perfect mathematical triangle, known as the **Epipolar Plane**.

## The Epipolar Constraint
If Camera 1 sees point $X$ at pixel $x_1$, Camera 2 *cannot* see point $X$ just anywhere on its sensor. Because of the Epipolar Plane, the projection of $X$ onto Camera 2 must fall along a specific, predictable 2D line called the **Epipolar Line**.

If your SIFT feature matcher pairs $x_1$ with a pixel in Camera 2 that does NOT sit exactly on this line, the match violates the laws of physics and is instantly deleted as an outlier.

## The Essential Matrix (E)
The relationship between $x_1$ and $x_2$ is governed by the **Essential Matrix**, which encapsulates the rotation and translation between the two cameras.

The mathematical constraint is elegant and absolute:
$$x_2^T \cdot E \cdot x_1 = 0$$

If you provide the computer with 8 good feature matches, it can solve this equation to discover $E$. Once $E$ is known, it can be mathematically decomposed to yield the Rotation ($R$) and Translation ($t$) of the camera.
