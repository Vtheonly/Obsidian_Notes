## 4.3 Feature Scaling and Normalization
Neural Networks initialize with tiny random weights (e.g., 0.01). If you feed them Wind Speed ranging from $0$ to $25$, and Energy Flux ranging from $0$ to $15,000$, the massive numbers will overwhelm the tiny weights. The gradients will either "explode" (`loss: nan`) or "vanish," preventing the model from learning.

### The Scalers
1.  **MinMaxScaler:** Compresses all data into a strict range between $0.0$ and $1.0$.
2.  **StandardScaler:** Centers data at a mean of $0$ with a standard deviation of $1$.
3.  **RobustScaler (Our Choice in Final Iteration):** Wind data is full of massive outliers (e.g., extreme hurricane gusts, sensor glitches, or turbine shutdowns). Standard scalers (MinMax, Standard) get easily ruined by these outliers. The `RobustScaler` uses the Interquartile Range (the middle 50% of the data) to scale the features, ensuring that sensor glitches do not distort the entire dataset and allow the model to learn stable patterns.

**Crucial Note:** You must save the exact Scaler object (`scaler.pkl`) used during training. During inference (production), you must apply this exact same scaler to incoming data, and use it to inversely transform the AI's $0.0 - 1.0$ prediction back into real megawatts (MW).
