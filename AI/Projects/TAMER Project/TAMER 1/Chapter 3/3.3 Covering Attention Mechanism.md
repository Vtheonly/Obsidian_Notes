# 3.3 Covering Attention Mechanism

Standard attention mechanisms sometimes suffer from "over-parsing" (reading the same symbol twice) or "under-parsing" (skipping small symbols like `-`, `.`, or `\prime`). To fix this, TAMER uses a **Coverage Attention Mechanism**.

## 📍 The "Dropped Symbol" Problem

In a dense mathematical expression, dots, commas, and small primes are easily overlooked by a standard attention head. Conversely, a large integral sign might be attended to multiple times, causing the model to output `\int \int` for a single symbol.

## 🛡️ The Coverage Vector

The Coverage Attention Mechanism tracks the history of where the model has already looked.

1.  **Initialization:** We maintain a Coverage Vector $C_t$, initialized as zeros.
2.  **Update:** At each decoding step $t$, the attention weights $\alpha_t$ are calculated.
3.  **Accumulation:** The Coverage Vector is updated: $C_t = C_{t-1} + \alpha_t$

### The Math of Adjusted Attention
During the next step, the Coverage Vector is explicitly injected into the attention score calculation:

$$e_{t, i} = v^T \tanh(W_q s_t + W_k h_i + W_c C_{t, i})$$

Where $W_c$ is a learnable weight for the coverage scores. If a pixel has a high coverage score (it has already been read), the network learns to penalize attending to it again.

## 💡 Why it's essential for HMER

Coverage Attention forces the network to sweep the entire image systematically. It ensures that every stroke is accounted for exactly once before the model emits the `<eos>` (End of Sequence) token.

⚠️ **Warning:** If your model outputs `x + y` when the image clearly says `x + y + z`, your coverage weight $W_c$ might be too low, or your attention mechanism is "dropping focus."

---
> [!TIP]
> **Points Students Often Miss:** Coverage isn't just about avoiding repetition. It also helps the model know **when to stop**. If the coverage vector shows that 99% of the ink has been "read," the model is much more likely to predict the `<eos>` token correctly.
